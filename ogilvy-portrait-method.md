# Ogilvy Portrait Method — Extended Reference

## The Source of the Discipline

Ogilvy described his own research process in Confessions of an Advertising Man: before writing a single word for any client, he would spend weeks studying the product, the competition, and above all the consumer. He kept a portrait of the consumer on his desk while writing — not a demographic chart, but a fully imagined person whose intelligence and taste he respected.

His famous instruction to Rolls-Royce copywriters was not "write about the car." It was "write to the kind of person who buys this car — what does he care about, what does he fear, what does he believe about himself, what would make him feel this purchase was the right one?" The copy followed naturally from knowing the person.

The Prospect Portrait applies this discipline to direct response lead magnet creation. The difference from Ogilvy's brand advertising context: in direct response, the portrait must also specify the exact moment of encounter — the emotional state, the trigger, the competing thoughts — because the copy must work in that specific moment or not at all.

## Worked Prospect Portraits by Niche

### Portrait 1: Online Coach — Client Acquisition Lead Magnet

**Demographic Shell:**
Female, 32–44, works from home, 1–3 years into coaching business, earning $2k–$6k/month inconsistently, previously in corporate role or similar profession

**Daily Reality:**
Wakes early, checks email and DMs hoping for new inquiries before the day starts — rarely finds them. Blocks off client call time and spends remaining hours on content creation, admin, and the persistent background anxiety of not knowing where next month's income is coming from. Consumes content on Instagram and YouTube primarily, often late at night after family obligations.

**Fear Architecture:**
- Primary fear: "My business won't be sustainable and I'll have to go back to a job"
- Secondary fears: Appearing unprofessional, being exposed as someone who doesn't have it together, having made the wrong career decision
- Fear they won't admit: "Maybe I'm just not good enough at the business side to make this work"
- Fear trigger: Seeing a peer post about a "sold-out month" or "5 new clients this week"

**Frustration History:**
Year 1: Relied entirely on referrals, got 2–3 clients. Year 2: Started posting daily on Instagram, got some engagement, got 1 client directly from social. Bought a "client attraction" course for $497 — got general advice that didn't translate to results. Tried cold outreach, felt uncomfortable, abandoned it after 2 weeks. Currently at 18 months with no reliable system, alternating between okay months and panicked months.

**Self-Perception Gap:**
- Current: "I'm a good coach who doesn't have the business side figured out yet"
- Identity threat: "I'm someone who isn't capable of running a real business"
- Aspirational: "I'm a professional with a business that grows predictably while I focus on my actual work"

**Conversation in Their Head (11pm, Instagram, just saw a peer's success post):**
> "She got five new clients this month. Again. How is she doing that? I'm posting just as much as she does. Okay, I know I shouldn't compare but... I genuinely don't understand what I'm missing. Let me just scroll for a few minutes. Wait, this lead magnet thing — I've downloaded like three of these. But this one looks different, it's specifically about... okay it's talking about getting leads without posting every day. That's exactly what I want to stop doing. Let me just read this for a minute."

**Language Fingerprint:**
- Problem: "feast and famine", "screaming into the void", "posting every day and getting nowhere", "I don't have a system", "chasing clients"
- Desire: "wake up to new inquiries", "consistent", "without having to chase", "predictable", "focus on the actual coaching"
- Skepticism: "I've tried everything", "generic advice", "this won't work for me specifically", "show me someone like me who it worked for"
- Voice register: Conversational, self-aware, occasionally self-deprecating, shorter sentences when frustrated

**Media & Influence Environment:**
Instagram (heavy), YouTube (occasional deep dives), podcast listener (business/mindset). Follows 3–5 prominent business coaches. Has been exposed to: Amy Porterfield, Jenna Kutcher, and niche-specific coaches. Pre-existing belief: "building an email list is important but I haven't done it properly yet."

**Buying History:**
Has purchased: 1–2 courses ($300–$600 each), possibly a group program. Has found partial value but not transformative results. Current posture: cautious — will read carefully before opting in, looking for specificity and proof from someone who looks like her. Proof requirement: HIGH.

**Moment of Maximum Receptivity:**
Late evening (9–11pm), on her phone, after a day that felt unproductive on the business side. Just saw a peer's success post or just finished a client call that reminded her how good she is at the work — which made the contrast with her business struggles more acute.

**Copy Anchor Statement:**
> "I've been coaching for almost two years and I genuinely love the work — but I've never cracked how to get clients consistently. I post, I show up, I put in the effort, and some months I'm fine and some months I'm quietly panicking. I've bought courses. I've tried things. Nothing has been the one thing that actually works reliably. I'm not looking for someone to tell me to 'build my audience' or 'show up authentically.' I want a specific system and I want to see proof it worked for someone who looks like me."

### Portrait 2: New Mom — Postpartum Weight Loss Lead Magnet

**Demographic Shell:**
Female, 28–40, 4–18 months postpartum, primary caregiver, working or recently returned to work, conscious of body changes, limited time

**Daily Reality:**
Days structured around the baby's schedule rather than her own. Sleep-deprived. Grabs food when she can, often skipping meals and then overeating. Has 15–20 minutes of personal time, usually used for phone scrolling rather than exercise. Consuming content on Instagram and TikTok during feeding sessions and after bedtime.

**Fear Architecture:**
- Primary fear: "My body is permanently changed and won't go back"
- Secondary fears: Husband finding her less attractive, not fitting into clothes she loves, being judged by other moms who "bounced back"
- Fear they won't admit: "I'm afraid this is just what I look like now and I have to accept it"
- Fear trigger: Seeing "bounced back" posts from celebrities or peers with newborns younger than hers

**Self-Perception Gap:**
- Current: "I'm a mom whose body went through something huge and I'm struggling to find myself again"
- Identity threat: "I'm someone who let herself go"
- Aspirational: "I'm someone who feels like herself — not a before photo, just comfortable and present in my body"

**Conversation in Their Head:**
> "I'm so tired of feeling this way. I know my body just had a baby and I should give it grace but... it's been almost a year. I've tried cutting calories but I'm breastfeeding and I don't know what's safe. I tried exercising and I just have no energy for it. This lead magnet says something about postpartum specifically — most of these things are just generic weight loss stuff repackaged. Let me see if this is actually different."

**Copy Anchor Statement:**
> "It's been almost a year since my daughter was born and I still don't feel like myself. I know I should be patient but I'm exhausted and I miss feeling comfortable in my own body. Everything I've tried feels like it was designed for someone without a baby — the meal plans don't account for breastfeeding, the workout schedules assume I have an hour free every day, and nobody talks about the fact that everything is just harder when you're running on four hours of sleep. I'm not looking to be thin. I just want to feel normal again."

## The Internal Monologue Test

Ogilvy's personal test for every piece of copy: read it aloud as if you are the consumer, in the voice of the consumer, in their moment of encounter. Does it feel true? Does it feel like something that would stop you?

The internal monologue dimension of the Prospect Portrait makes this test operational. Before running any copy skill, the agent should re-read the internal monologue for the specific moment of encounter, then ask: does this copy interrupt and redirect that monologue toward belief? If not, it is not working yet.

The monologue is not a rhetorical device. It is a diagnostic tool — the most precise and practical check against copy that sounds right in the abstract but fails in the real moment of encounter.
