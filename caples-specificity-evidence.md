# Caples Specificity Evidence — Extended Reference

## The Test Data

John Caples spent his career at BBDO running split tests on direct mail and print advertising before split testing had a name. His most famous test compared two headlines for a music correspondence course:

**Headline A:** "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?"
**Headline B:** "Improve Your English"

Headline A outperformed Headline B by 1,900%. The difference: A was specific (implies a test, a defined error, a definable condition) while B was directional (a vague improvement).

His conclusion: *"The difference between a specific statement and a general statement is often the difference between an advertisement that pays and one that does not."*

He also tested:
- "Earn Money at Home" vs. "Earn $500 a Month at Home Writing Short Paragraphs"
  → The specific version outperformed by a factor of 4
- "Reduce Your Weight" vs. "Lose 11 Pounds in 4 Weeks"
  → Specific version: consistently higher response
- "Play Piano" vs. "Play Piano in 30 Days or Your Money Back"
  → Specific + guaranteed: dramatic lift

The pattern across thousands of tests was consistent: specific language converts. General language generates impression and forgets.

## The Psychology of Specificity

Why does specific outperform general? Three mechanisms:

**1. Specificity signals truth**
Vague language is the language of protection — someone covering themselves because they're not sure. Specific language is the language of reporting — someone who has been there and is telling you what happened. The brain reads them differently. Vague triggers evaluation ("is this trustworthy?"). Specific triggers imagination ("is this true for me?"). Imagination converts at a higher rate than evaluation.

**2. Specificity creates a mental image**
"Lose weight" produces no picture. "Lose 11 lbs in 21 days" produces a picture: the scale, the date, the specific number. Mental images engage the prospect. Abstract claims produce no engagement.

**3. Specificity implies mechanism**
A specific outcome implies a specific process that produced it. "34% opt-in rate" implies that whoever made this claim had a specific method that produced a measurable result. The reader infers credibility from precision even without being shown the method explicitly.

## The Irregular Number Effect

Caples noticed that irregular numbers — numbers that don't end in 0 or 5 — consistently produced higher belief than round numbers, even when the round number was the more accurate one.

**Why:** Round numbers read as estimates. Irregular numbers read as measurements. "30%" looks like someone rounded from a real number. "34%" looks like someone measured a real number.

When reporting your own data, always use the actual measured number, not a rounded version.

When you don't have actual data and must use estimates:
- Use ranges rather than point estimates: "typically 20–35%" rather than "about 25%"
- Use qualifiers that explain the range: "varies between 15% and 40% depending on traffic source"
- Use conservative lower bounds: "at minimum 2–3 per week" rather than "up to 10 per week"

Optimistic upper bounds that aren't reliably achieved destroy trust when the prospect's experience falls short. Conservative lower bounds that are reliably exceeded build trust when the prospect outperforms expectations.

## Specificity and the Believability Ceiling

Caples also documented a failure mode of over-specificity: numbers so large or so precise that they signal fabrication rather than measurement.

**Signs you've hit the ceiling:**
- The number requires the prospect to fundamentally revise their existing beliefs to find it plausible
- The number is more precise than the mechanism could plausibly produce ("34.7%")
- The claim is so specific that it applies to almost no one ("for left-handed graphic designers born after 1985 who own a Mac")

At the believability ceiling, the right move is to add a qualifying condition that makes the claim simultaneously more humble and more credible:

"Achieve a 34% opt-in rate" → feels high for a skeptic
"Achieve a 25–34% opt-in rate when your copy matches the awareness level of your traffic source" → specific, conditioned, and believable
